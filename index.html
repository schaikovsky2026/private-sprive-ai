Approach

Risk management is the only edge.

Predicting which stocks will rise next month is, by all available evidence, beyond the reach of most active managers. Predicting when a sustained selloff has confirmed — and stepping aside — is not. The strategy commits to the second discipline and abandons the first.

01

No stock picking

The portfolio is either SPY or cash — never individual securities. That removes the entire class of "wrong-stock" risk and means the long-run return is the index return, modified only by when you are in and when you are out.

02

No survivorship bias

Because the strategy holds the index itself, there is no question of "which companies got included in the test." Every dollar of the backtest could have been earned by every investor with a brokerage account, in every year, on the unadjusted public S&P 500.

03

Sparse, mechanical signals

The exit and re-entry rules are deterministic. Across thirty-three years and 392 monthly observations, the model traded only twelve times. The cost of being wrong is bounded — you cannot misjudge a trade you do not make.

04

Designed for the bad years

The strategy is not built to beat SPY in calm markets — it usually doesn't. It is built to survive 2000-style and 2008-style drawdowns, where index investors lost half their capital and many never psychologically recovered. The historical numbers show that role is exactly what it has fulfilled.

The mechanism in three layers

Layer 1 · Denoise

Kalman filter

The monthly SPY adjusted-close price is run through a 2-state Kalman filter (position + velocity) before any signal is computed. This removes the month-to-month noise that would otherwise produce false trade signals.

Layer 2 · Trend

9-month SMA

A nine-month simple moving average is computed on the Kalman-filtered price. The nine-month horizon is long enough to ignore corrections, short enough to identify a real regime change before the bottom is in.

Layer 3 · Confirm

2σ exit band

The model exits SPY for cash only when the Kalman price closes below the 9-month SMA minus two standard deviations. Re-entry occurs when price reclaims the moving average. The 2σ threshold is what produces twelve trades in thirty-three years instead of two hundred.

All parameters were specified before backtesting. The 9-month window, 2σ band, and Kalman filter settings (R%=0.1, Q%=0.01) were not optimized against the historical data — they are the published defaults from the trading literature and the underlying open-source library. Curve-fitting would invalidate the test; nothing about the parameter choice was reverse-engineered from the results.

The drawdown number is the headline

A −50.8% peak-to-trough loss — which is what SPY did during the 2008 financial crisis — is the kind of event that ends investing careers. Many investors who held SPY through 2007–2009 sold at the bottom and never reinvested. The VolBand model's −27.5% maximum drawdown is still painful, but it is recoverable. A 46% reduction in worst-case loss is, for most investors, more valuable than a 0.7 pp/yr return advantage. The two combined is rare.

Engagement

How the strategy is delivered.

A signal subscription, not a fund. Your capital and custody remain entirely with you.

01

Monthly Signal

On the first trading day of each month, you receive the current state: in SPY or in cash. The signal flips roughly once every three years on average — most months, the message is simply "no change."

02

Capital Stays Yours

You hold SPY (or any S&P 500 equivalent — VOO, IVV) and cash equivalent (SGOV, BIL, money market) in your own brokerage account. The service never holds, custodies, or directs your funds.

03

Twelve Trades · Thirty-Three Years

The historical signal switched twelve times across the full backtest. That is roughly one trade every three years. Execution friction, tax drag, and behavioral cost are all minimized by design.
